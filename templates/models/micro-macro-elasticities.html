Substitutability in the General Equilibrium

Recent debates around the energy crisis and possible consequences of a boycott against Russia have brought the economic concept of elasticities into the spotlight. As with many economic terms, it is used differently (or at least more multifariously) in the economics profession than in the general public. With the following diagram, I aim at intuitively presenting some important aspects from the perspective of economists.

The concept of elasticity is closely related to that of substitutability: The higher the substitutability of a good or input in consumption or production, the higher is the respective elasticity of demand (faced with a price increase, it is easier to switch to another commodity). This is mainly discussed at the firm or industry level, which was coined the "engineering view" by Christian Bayer, co-author of a much-discussed study on the effects of a gas boycott (about which I have written here). For example, it is often stressed that the chemical industry, at least in the short term, is completely depended on gas as an input, and would have to shut down in case of a gas shortage (zero substitutability, i.e. inelastic demand). But how does this translate into GDP-losses at the national level?

Let's do what economists do best and simplify and assume: Imagine an economy where 50% of firms have such a production technology that they cannot produce without gas. The most simple production function for this case would be \(Y = min \{ E,L \} \), with E as energy and L as labour. The other 50% of firms do not use gas and only produce with labour: \(Y = L \). In the below figure, these input mixes are depicted by the blue GD (gas-dependent) and orange GI (gas-independent) lines in the right panel. Note how labour demand in the gas-dependent sector changes with the energy supply (\( E^S \)). On the left panel, we depict a simple labour market with a strong simplification: Here, the GD-sector buys as much labour as it needs before the GI-sector purchases its labour inputs. Since wages equal unit labour costs in the GI-sector, it only buys labour up to the point where wages equal unit prices for its goods (\( w^*_2 = P_2 \), again assuming a perfectly competitive setting).

What happens in case of an energy supply reduction? In an ideal world, the GD-sector would lay off workers, which reduces prices (wages) on the labour market, so that the GI-sector compensates for these layoffs with higher purchases (employment). If the GI-sector could grow without any hurdles, there would be no GDP loss at all. Despite half of its firms having zero elasticity of substitution with respect to gas, the whole economy is here perfectly elastic! This is caused by general equilibrium effects, i.e., the reallocation of resources (here labour) driven by market mechanisms (here wages). Of course, the GI-industries do have growth restrictions such as capacity (especially in the short run) or demand limitations (also relevant in the long run). Also, in reality the GD firms produce inputs needed by the GI sector (cascade effects via supply chains). Depending on the magnitude of these frictions, the overall economic outcome looks very different, up to the worst case where GI-industries do not grow in response to the energy shortages (\( g_{GI} = 0 \)) and the whole economy shrinks by as much as the GD-sector.

Therefore, one must never confuse partial equilibrium outcomes in certain sectors with the effects for the whole economy. While it is important to know about the technical substitution possibilities on the micro level (plants and firms), one should also think about possible general equilibrium channels: Can other sectors of the economy make use of layed-off resources? How much of the production losses can be compensated by imports (especially relevant for intermediary goods)? What is relevant for the short run (6-12 months) and what only for the long run (2-3 years)? These are the questions that economists ask (and assume answers about) in their model calculations.

Mapping Sector Balances

As in personal life, identities play an important role in economics. Here, an identity is a statement that is by design true. They are very useful because they show the implications of indisputable mechanisms and provide insights that to not depend on any (potentially faulty) theory. An identity that is especially relevant for economic policy making are sector balances: If we divide the whole world into domestic private sector, domestic government sector, and the rest of the world (ROW), then the sum of monetary flows between those three sectors must be zero. Each time someone spends money, someone else receives it; and each time someone spends more than he earns (deficits), someone else must earn more than they spend (surplus).

We can write the above-described identity as PB + FB - TB = 0 (private balance + fiscal balance + trade balance), taking into account that the trade balance of a country is the mirror image of the balance of the rest of the world (if TB > 0, there are more flows from ROW to the domestic economy than the other way around, i.e., it is a deficit for ROW). This becomes clearer when we rearrange the formula to PB = TB – FB. For the domestic private sector to be in surplus, the country must either achieve a trade balance or the government must transfer more money to firms and households as it receives in taxes (fiscal deficit). Of course, it is also crucial not the forget another identity: Since the whole world is a closed system, a country’s trade surplus implies a trade deficit somewhere else (and, thereby, an accumulation of claims in the respective country and an accumulation of debt somewhere else).

Usually, policy makers do not want the private sector to run a deficit over a prolonged period, since this is a sure way into an economic crisis as it drains demand (and electorates are not keen on having their wealth slowly eaten up). At the same time, running a permanent trade surplus makes it harder for other countries to allow their private sector to be in surplus and can create political tensions (although trade deficits are not per se bad, see here). Consequently, a fiscal deficit is often needed to maintain a private sector surplus. The below sector balances map (proposed and explained by Robert Parentau 2010) depicts those mechanisms. A country's position on the map is a reflection of the state of its economy and the economic model it pursues (see here to understand the developments for European countries in that regard).

Analysing drivers of GDP growth

First things first: Of course, GDP growth is no measure for holistic development. Not at all. And yes, GDP growth should not be an objective for itself, but only a means to another end. This is why the Degrowth debate is important, as it kick-starts a discussion about what we actually aim for as a society. And needless to say, if we do not manage to achieve GDP growth while staying within the planetary boundaries, it is better not to grow. Nevertheless, GDP growth still is one relevant indicator to assess the economic capabilities of a country. But how did and do countries actually grow?

To give a purely descriptive answer, it is useful to decompose GDP growth into the contribution of multiple factors. For this, one can write "GDP = (GDP / hours worked) * (hours worked / employment) * (employment / labor force) * (labor force / population) * population". In a next step, one calculates how GDP growth is reflected in the growth of the separate factors, namely productivity, average hours per employee, employment rate, participation rate, and population growth. It is important to note that this does not tell us anything about the causes of GDP growth. For example, average hours per employee can change because people want to work less (and enjoy more leisure) or because the order situation for firms is not good enough to have all employees work as much as they would like to. Nevertheless, one can learn quite a bit from the descriptive pictures below, also because a causal explanation for a country's growth must match the empirical facts. If people explain low overall growth with an increase in size of low-wage sectors, where productivity is low, this should be reflected in low productivity growth but stable/increasing employment (be it total employment or hours worked).

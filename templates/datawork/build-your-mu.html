Test your own Monetary Union

There exists a large literature about the concept of "Optimal Currency Areas" as introduced in a famous article by Robert Mundell in 1961. This literare aims at identifying criteria against which one can assess existing or hypothetical monetary unions, i.e. union of countries with a single currency (and, therefore, a common monetary policy). In the end, it all boils down to comparing costs and benefits of such a monetary union: Costs arise from the loss of national monetary policy and shrinking space for national fiscal policy (due to the inability to directly monetize deficits), which is a problem when different members of the monetary union are at different points in their business cycle (e.g., because of asymmetric shocks). On the other side, benefits stem from lower transaction costs and lower uncertainty for cross country trade, since exchange rates are now perfectly fixed (to 1). Costs from monetary integration are lowest when business cycle between countries are highly synchronized, benefits are highest when the members of the monetary union trade a lot with each others.

The following tool lets you select a number of countries (the default setting selects all countries from the current Eurozone for which sufficient data was available) and depicts the above cost-benefit-analysis: For assessing potential benefits, the horizontal axis shows the share of total exports going to the selected union for each country (data from WITS), while the horizontal axis shows the correlation of the national output gap with the average output gap for the selected union (data from AMECO). Note that the mean output gap is calculated without weights for the size of the respective economies. The higher the lines are, the lower are the costs from weakening national policies, the further the lines are to the right, the higher the benefits are for cross-member trade.
